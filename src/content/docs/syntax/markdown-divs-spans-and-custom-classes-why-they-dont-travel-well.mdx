## What matters first

Divs, spans, and custom classes inside Markdown are usually a sign that layout concerns are leaking into the content layer.

They can work in controlled publishing stacks, but they do not travel well across platforms because:

- sanitization varies,
- CSS classes only mean something in one stack,
- and the source becomes harder to reuse.

## Why this matters

At first, adding a small wrapper feels harmless. Over time, pages accumulate:

- `<div class="note">`
- `<span class="highlight">`
- custom wrappers for alignment, spacing, or layout

At that point, the Markdown file is no longer just content. It is partially coupled to a theme.

## When wrappers may be acceptable

They may be acceptable when:

- the renderer is known,
- the class names are part of a stable system,
- and the content is not expected to move elsewhere unchanged.

That is a controlled publishing decision.

## When wrappers are the wrong answer

They are usually the wrong answer when:

- the site needs portable Markdown,
- the same wrappers are copied manually across many pages,
- or the classes are compensating for a missing component or template.

That is where source debt grows fastest.

## The practical rule

If the same div or span pattern appears more than a few times, it probably belongs in:

- a component,
- a shortcode/directive,
- or the publishing layer,

not in raw Markdown source.

## Cleaner alternatives

Before adding another custom wrapper, consider whether the problem belongs somewhere else:

- use a documented component for notes, warnings, tabs, or cards;
- use frontmatter when the content needs metadata rather than inline decoration;
- use a template when the same layout repeats across many pages;
- use plain Markdown when the styling is not essential to understanding.

This keeps the source readable and makes migrations less painful. A wrapper is defensible when it represents a stable content pattern. It is weak when it only patches a visual preference that should live in the theme.
